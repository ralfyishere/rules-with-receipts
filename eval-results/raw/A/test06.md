Short version: **don't send that message yet.** The conversion-rate gap is real data, but the conclusion "move the entire budget to C" doesn't follow from it. Here are the holes I'd want closed first.

**1. Conversion rate isn't the objective — cost per acquisition is.**
A 12% conversion rate is worthless if C costs 10× more per click/impression. What you actually want to compare is **cost per conversion** (spend ÷ conversions), or ideally profit per dollar spent. It's entirely possible A converts at 2% but at a lower CPA than C. Pull the cost side before deciding anything.

**2. The 12% is an *average* rate at current spend, not the *marginal* rate at 5× spend.**
Channels saturate. C is probably converting at 12% because it's currently reaching its best-fit, highest-intent slice of audience. Pour the whole budget in and you buy the next-best audience, then the next — marginal conversion rate falls, often sharply. You're implicitly assuming 12% holds at any spend level, and it almost never does. C may also simply lack the inventory/audience volume to absorb the full budget.

**3. Are A and B feeding C?**
This is the big one. If A/B are top-of-funnel (prospecting, awareness) and C is bottom-of-funnel (retargeting, branded search), then C's 12% partly exists *because* A and B warmed those people up. Kill A/B and C's rate can collapse — you'd be cutting the roots to water the flower. Check whether C's traffic is net-new or recycled from A/B, and how attribution is assigned (last-click flatters closer channels like C).

**4. Is 12% even real, or small-sample noise?**
If C's rate is based on a few hundred visitors and a handful of conversions, the confidence interval is wide — 12% could really be 6%. How much volume is each rate built on?

**5. Same conversion = same value?**
If C's conversions are lower order value or worse retention/LTV, a higher rate can still be worse revenue.

**What I'd tell the team instead:** "C shows a strong conversion rate — I want to shift *more* budget toward it and test how far we can push it before returns drop, while keeping A/B funded enough to confirm whether they feed C." That's defensible. "All-in on C" is a bet on four unverified assumptions.
