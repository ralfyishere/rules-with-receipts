**Don't send it yet — the logic has a hole big enough to burn the budget through.** Conversion *rate* alone can't justify moving spend, and the one number you're optimizing is the one most likely to collapse the moment you act on it.

Here's the core problem and the checks to run first.

## The fatal flaw: rates don't survive scaling
The 12% is an **average** over C's *current* traffic. When you pour the whole budget into C, you buy its *next*, colder audience — broader targeting, higher bid competition, more top-of-funnel clicks. Marginal conversion rate is almost always below average conversion rate. You could easily 5× spend on C and watch it settle at 4%. The relevant question isn't "what does C convert at today" but "what will the *next dollar* into C convert at" — a number you don't have yet.

## Why C probably isn't 6× better
A 12% vs 2% gap usually isn't channel *quality* — it's **audience intent**, i.e. composition, not causation:
- **C is likely bottom-funnel.** Branded search, retargeting, email to existing users convert high *because the person already decided*. A and B (cold social, display, prospecting) create the demand that later converts as a "C" conversion.
- **Kill A/B and you may starve C.** If last-touch attribution is crediting C for demand that A/B generated, cutting them doesn't redirect their budget — it removes the top of C's funnel. C's 12% then drops too.

## Five checks before you tell the team
1. **Volume + CAC, not just rate.** What's each channel's conversion *count* and *cost per conversion*? A 12% rate on 200 visits loses to 2% on 500,000. Optimize profit per dollar, not rate.
2. **Same denominator?** Is "conversion" defined identically across A, B, C — same event, same visitor definition? Mismatched metrics fake the gap.
3. **Sample size.** Is 12% from 40 conversions or 4? Small-n rates are noise; check if the difference is even statistically real.
4. **Attribution model.** Last-touch vs multi-touch changes this entire picture. Run it both ways.
5. **Saturation / can C even absorb it?** Many high-intent channels (branded search, retargeting pools) are volume-capped — you literally can't spend the budget there without expanding to worse traffic.

## What to do instead
Don't reallocate 100% off a snapshot. **Shift budget incrementally** (e.g. +30% to C) and watch whether C's *marginal* conversion rate and CAC hold. If they do, shift more. If A/B turn out to feed C, you'll see C's rate sag as you starve them — cheap to detect at 30%, catastrophic to discover at 100%.

**One-line version for your gut check:** you're treating an average as a marginal rate, and a correlation (high-intent audiences land on C) as a lever (C makes people convert). Neither survives contact with the reallocation.

The honest uncertainty: I can't tell you C is a *bad* bet — it may genuinely deserve more. I can tell you the current logic doesn't establish that, and the failure mode (rate collapses on scale, A/B were feeding C) is the common one. The volume/CAC numbers and attribution model would settle it.
