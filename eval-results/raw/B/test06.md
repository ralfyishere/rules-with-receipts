Your logic has a hole big enough to torch the budget through. Don't send that message yet. Here's the sanity check.

## The core error
You're optimizing conversion **rate** as if it were the goal. It isn't. The goal is conversions (or revenue, or profit) **per dollar**. Rate alone tells you nothing about that, because it ignores three things: volume, cost, and *why* the rates differ.

## The five things that will bite you

**1. Rate ≠ marginal rate.** That 12% is the *average* of C's current spend, on C's current audience. When you pour 10x the budget in, you buy the *next*, less-qualified slice of that audience — marginal conversion falls, often hard. You'd be projecting a small-scale average onto a large-scale reality. The 2% channels might also be near their floor, but you can't assume C stays at 12%.

**2. Volume/ceiling.** What if C converts at 12% because it's tiny and pre-qualified — 200 leads from branded search or retargeting? A and B at 2% on 40,000 leads each may still produce more total conversions. Moving budget to a channel that can't absorb it just means wasted or rising-cost spend.

**3. You're ignoring cost per click/lead.** A 2% channel with dirt-cheap traffic can have a *lower* cost per acquisition than a 12% channel with expensive traffic. Compute **cost per conversion** (and ideally CAC vs. LTV) per channel. That's the number that decides budget, not conversion %.

**4. Channels aren't independent — attribution.** If A/B are top-of-funnel (awareness) and C is bottom-of-funnel (retargeting/branded), then A and B are *feeding* C. Under last-touch attribution, C gets credit for demand A and B created. Kill A/B and C's 12% can collapse because there's nothing left to retarget.

**5. Concentration risk.** 100% in one channel = one algorithm change, ad-account suspension, or audience fatigue away from zero pipeline. Even if C is genuinely best, all-in is fragile.

## Also worth a glance
Sample size — is 12% vs 2% built on enough *conversions* to be real, or is C's rate a small-n mirage?

## What to actually do
- Pull **cost per conversion** and **total conversions** per channel, not just rate.
- Check whether A/B are assist/upper-funnel for C (look at multi-touch paths, or run a holdout: pause A/B for a subset and watch what happens to C).
- Shift budget **incrementally** toward C and measure whether the *marginal* conversion rate holds. Reallocate on evidence, not on a single ratio.

The defensible message to the team is "C looks efficient — let's shift budget toward it in stages and verify it scales," not "all-in on C." The first is a smart bet; the second is a guess wearing a percentage.
