This is a two-line message with almost no content, so let me not over-engineer it. The main problem: "the thing from last week" is vague, and there's no deadline, which is exactly why follow-ups get ignored. Here are a few sharper versions depending on what you're after.

**If you just want to name the thing (drop-in replacement):**
> Hey — following up on [the Q3 budget doc / the intro to Sarah / etc.] from last week. Any update on where that stands? No rush, but let me know by Friday if you can. Thanks!

**Tighter and more direct:**
> Hey — circling back on [X]. Where does this stand? Happy to help move it along if anything's blocking you.

**If you want to make it easy to say yes:**
> Hey — quick follow-up on [X] from last week. Is that still on your radar, or should I check back later? Either way works, just want to make sure it doesn't fall through.

The three things that make a follow-up actually get answered:

1. **Name the thing.** "The thing from last week" forces them to reconstruct context; a concrete noun ("the contract draft," "your feedback on the deck") lets them act immediately.
2. **Give a soft deadline.** "When you get a chance" has no edge, so it slides. "By Friday" or "before our next sync" creates a gentle prompt without pressure.
3. **Lower the cost of replying.** Offering an out ("or should I check back later?") or help ("happy to unblock anything") often gets a faster response than a bare ask.
