**Don't send that yet — the logic has a hole big enough to burn your whole budget through.** Conversion *rate* alone can't justify moving 100% of spend. Here's what it's hiding.

## The core problem
"C converts at 12%" is a statement about the *traffic C currently gets*, not about *the channel's capacity to absorb budget*. You're about to change the second thing based on the first. Those come apart in several ways:

**1. Cost isn't in the equation.** Conversion rate ≠ profit. If C is retargeting or branded search, each conversion may cost 5–10× an A/B conversion. The right metric is **cost per acquisition** (or ROAS / margin per dollar), not conversion %. A 2% channel at $2 CPCs can easily beat a 12% channel at $40 CPCs. You cannot make this call without the cost side.

**2. The 12% won't survive scaling.** C likely converts high *because it's small and self-selected* — it's catching people already far down the funnel (searched your brand, clicked a retargeting ad, etc.). Pour 10× budget in and you buy colder, broader traffic. The rate falls, often hard. Marginal conversion rate at 10× spend, not current average rate, is what matters — and you have zero data on it because you've never spent that much there.

**3. Sample size.** If C's 12% comes from 50 visitors, that's 6 conversions — the true rate could plausibly be 4% or 20%. Before betting the budget, check whether the gap is statistically real or just a small-numbers artifact. (Rough gut check: how many conversions is 12% actually built on?)

**4. Attribution / funnel role.** A and B may be *creating* the demand that C harvests. If they run awareness/discovery and C catches people at the bottom, killing A and B starves the top of the funnel — and C's 12% quietly collapses because the primed audience stops arriving. Last-touch conversion rate systematically over-credits bottom-funnel channels.

**5. Absolute volume.** 2% of a large channel can be more total conversions than 12% of a tiny one. Rate tells you efficiency, not scale.

## Before you tell the team, get these four numbers
1. **Cost per conversion** (or ROAS) for each channel — the actual decision metric.
2. **Conversion counts** behind each rate — is 12% even solid?
3. **What C's traffic is** — branded/retargeting/bottom-funnel, or genuinely cold like A/B? If it's not apples-to-apples, the comparison is meaningless.
4. **Trend as C's spend rose historically** — does its rate hold or decay with volume?

## The safer move
Don't reallocate 100% off one metric. **Shift budget incrementally** — e.g. move 20–30% toward C and watch whether its conversion rate and CPA hold as volume climbs. If they do, shift more. If the rate craters at higher spend (likely), you've learned that cheaply instead of torching A and B on an assumption.

**Bottom line:** the 12% vs 2% gap is a real signal worth chasing, but "move everything" treats an efficiency ratio measured on today's traffic mix as if it were a guaranteed return at 10× the volume. Get cost-per-conversion and conversion counts first; then scale into C gradually rather than all at once.
