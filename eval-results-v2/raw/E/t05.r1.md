## Why the "ads clearly work" framing is risky

A CFO's first question will be: *how do you know it was the ad?* Right now, you don't — not to the standard of a $120k commitment. The gaps a CFO will find in about thirty seconds:

- **One week, no control.** A doubling in a single week could be the ad, or a confounder running in parallel. Was anything else live that week — a product launch, other campaigns, press, a seasonal bump, a viral post?
- **Absolute numbers matter.** Doubling from 40→80 signups is noise; 2,000→4,000 is signal. A percentage with no denominator won't survive scrutiny.
- **Free signups ≠ value.** The spend is justified by *paid conversions and LTV*, not free-tier registrations. Podcast-driven free signups often convert worse than organic. You have zero conversion data yet.
- **One-time audience vs. sustained reach.** A podcast's engaged listeners are a finite pool that hears the ad once. Week-one response is often the *peak*, not the run-rate. Scaling 15× ($8k → $120k) assumes linear returns that rarely hold.
- **Annual lock-in before CAC is known.** Signing 12 months now means committing before you know your cost-per-paid-customer. That's the specific thing CFOs are paid to prevent.

None of this means the ad *didn't* work — it may well have. It means the evidence doesn't yet support the size of the commitment, and a memo that claims it does will damage your credibility on the next ask.

## What I put in the memo instead

A justification to **scale spend deliberately**: a shorter/smaller initial commitment (e.g. quarterly or a 3-month flight) with defined success metrics tied to *paid* conversions, so you buy the information before you buy the year. If the network only offers annual, the memo asks for it with an exit/measurement checkpoint. This is easier to approve *and* easier to defend later.
