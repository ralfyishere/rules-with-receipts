## Recommendation: Don't take Dana off deploy duty — the data points at the *payments service*, not Dana

Taking Dana off deploys would almost certainly **not** reduce outages, because Dana isn't the cause. The outages track which *service* is being deployed, not who deploys it. Dana just happens to be the person most often assigned to the fragile one (payments).

### What the data actually shows

16 deploys, 6 outages. The headline claim ("most outages follow Dana's deploys") is technically true in raw count — but it's a textbook confounding trap.

**By engineer (raw — the misleading view):**

| Engineer | Deploys | Outages | Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dana | 6 | 3 | 50% |
| Alex | 6 | 2 | 33% |
| Sam | 4 | 1 | 25% |

Dana leads — so far it looks like leadership is right. But now split by service:

**By service (the real driver):**

| Service | Deploys | Outages | Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| payments | 6 | 5 | **83%** |
| web | 10 | 1 | **10%** |

Payments deploys fail ~83% of the time **regardless of who runs them**. Web deploys almost never fail. **5 of the 6 outages were payments deploys**; the 6th was Sam's, on web.

**Controlling for service — Dana is not the worst:**

| Engineer | payments rate | web rate |
|---|---|---|
| Dana | 3/4 (75%) | 0/2 (0%) |
| Alex | 2/2 (**100%**) | 0/4 (0%) |
| Sam | — | 1/4 (25%) |

Once you hold the service constant, Dana's payments failure rate (75%) is actually *lower* than Alex's (100%). Dana even owns the only payments deploy that *didn't* cause an outage (d109). Dana's web deploys have a perfect record.

Dana appears responsible only because Dana handled 4 of the 6 payments deploys. Reassign those same deploys to Alex and you'd expect *more* outages, not fewer.

### Draft note to leadership

> We looked at the deploy log (16 deploys, May–June). The raw numbers do show Dana on more outages than anyone else, but that's an artifact of assignment, not skill. Every outage but one was a **payments** deploy, and payments deploys fail ~83% of the time no matter who runs them — Alex's payments deploys actually failed at a higher rate than Dana's (100% vs 75%), and Dana's web deploys have never caused an outage. Removing Dana would shuffle the risky payments deploys to someone else and likely leave the outage rate unchanged or worse.
>
> The real problem is the payments deploy path — pipeline, tests, rollout process, or the service itself. Recommend we (1) keep Dana on deploys, and (2) open an investigation into why payments deploys are so fragile: add pre-deploy checks, staged rollout, or a deploy freeze on payments until we find the root cause.

### Caveats (state these to leadership)

- **Small sample:** 6 outages total. The direction is clear and the mechanism (payments is fragile) is strong, but exact per-person rates rest on 2–4 deploys each — don't over-read the precise percentages.
- The log shows *correlation* (outage yes/no per deploy), not root cause. It tells us *where* to look (payments), not *why* it breaks. That's the investigation to fund.
- I've assumed "outage=yes" means the deploy caused/coincided with the outage, as the framing implies.

Bottom line: the pattern leadership saw is real in the raw count but misattributed. The lever that reduces outages is fixing payments deploys, not moving Dana.
