## Why the current evidence doesn't support a $120k commit

- **n=1, no control.** One week, one ad, no comparison to a week where nothing ran. Weekly signups fluctuate on their own; a "doubling" off a normal-noise baseline is common. What was the prior 4-week range?
- **Attribution is unproven.** Did those signups actually come from the podcast? Unless you used a promo code, vanity URL, or signup survey, you're inferring the source from timing alone — and lots of things happen in a week (other channels, press, seasonality, organic).
- **Free signups ≠ the thing you're buying.** $120k has to be justified by *paid conversions, retention, and CAC payback* — not top-of-funnel free signups, which are the cheapest, least predictive metric. Did paid conversions move?
- **Linear extrapolation is the fatal flaw.** $8k → a spike does not mean $120k → 15× the spike. Ad response almost always shows diminishing returns; a single podcast's audience saturates. The 15th ad does not perform like the 1st.
- **Podcast ad effects decay.** A "week after" bump is exactly the pattern of a one-time spike that fades, not a durable acquisition channel.

**None of this means ads don't work.** It means you don't yet know, and $120k annual is a large, hard-to-reverse bet to place on a signal this thin.

## What I'd recommend instead

Don't ask the CFO to fund a conclusion — ask them to fund a **test that de-risks the conclusion**: a 3-month, ~$24–30k staged buy with proper attribution (unique promo codes/landing pages per drop) and a paid-conversion + CAC target agreed up front. If it hits, the $120k annual becomes an easy yes backed by real data. That's also a far easier memo to get approved.
